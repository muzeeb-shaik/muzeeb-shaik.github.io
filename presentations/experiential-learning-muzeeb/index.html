The Gender Gap: A Global Pattern

Global Picture

  • Women: only 35% of STEM graduates worldwide
  • In the US: 38.8% of science bachelor’s degrees
  • Only 26% of the US STEM workforce
  • Stakes: Substantial loss of productive capacity

India: Our Study Context

  • Women: 43% of total undergraduate enrollment
  • But only 29% in STEM disciplines
  • Fewer than 18% of scientists/engineers in labor force
  • Opportunity: Gender parity → 10–12 million more STEM workers

Today’s Talk

What if we could address gender stereotypes indirectly, by redesigning the learning experience itself?

Three Categories of Prior Interventions

1. Push Campaigns

  • Awareness campaigns
  • Mass communication
  • Public service announcements
  • Media messaging

Goal: Persuade individuals to reconsider stereotypes

(Paluck et al. 2021; Hermann et al. 2022)

2. Girls-Only Programs

  • After-school STEM activities
  • Mentoring programs
  • Interactions with STEM professionals
  • Separation as confidence-building

Goal: Close information gaps, build exposure

(Dubetz & Wilson 2013; Levine et al. 2015)

3. Gender-Congruent Environments

  • Female role models
  • Feminine classroom décor
  • Identity-affirming cues

Goal: Counteract masculine coding of science

(Master et al. 2016; Breda et al. 2023)

The Common Limitation

All three approaches explicitly position gender as the focal problem and attempt to address it through direct persuasion or identity-targeted messaging

The Problem with Direct Approaches

These interventions risk reactance:

Push Campaigns

  • May be dismissed as heavy-handed
  • Risk appearing tokenizing
  • Limited lasting impact on beliefs

Girls-Only Programs

  • Can reinforce the very group boundaries they seek to dismantle
  • Signal that girls require specialized support
  • Create parallel tracks rather than inclusive culture

Gender-Congruent Cues

  • Signal science requires special adaptation for women
  • Rather than science being a shared, gender-neutral domain

The Critical Gap

None of these approaches engage boys: the group that holds stronger gender-stereotypical beliefs and whose perceptions shape the social norms defining whether girls feel they belong in science

A Different Approach: Indirect Change

The Approach

Rather than targeting gender perceptions directly, what if we could produce gender-inclusive outcomes as a collateral benefit of redesigning the learning experience itself?

Research Questions

Primary: Can experiential science learning, designed for pedagogy not gender, simultaneously improve science knowledge and reduce gender stereotypes?

Secondary:

  • Does the intervention affect boys and girls differently?
  • Do effects extend to broader attitudes about gender and competence?

Theoretical Framework

How Can Experience Change Perceptions?

Marketing Insight: Shared Consumption

Research shows that co-consumption influences:

  • Social perceptions
  • Norm inference
  • Identity construction

(Ramanathan & McGill 2007; Jones & Campbell 2025)

Belief Updating Mechanism

When consumers observe others in shared experiences, they update their beliefs about:

  • Group capabilities and competencies
  • Category boundaries and membership
  • Social norms and expectations

The Application

When science learning is structured as shared experiential consumption, cross-gender collaboration triggers belief updating:

  • Students observe counter-stereotypical competence firsthand
  • Direct evidence challenges prior gender-science associations
  • Shared experience creates common identity around science

Why This Should Work: Intergroup Contact Theory

Allport (1954) & Pettigrew & Tropp (2006)

Core Principle

Positive, cooperative interaction between members of different social groups can reduce prejudice and stereotyping by fostering understanding and shared identity

Four Optimal Conditions for Contact

  • Equal status between groups in the situation
  • Common goals that require interdependence
  • Intergroup cooperation (not competition)
  • Institutional support from authorities

Why This Matters

When these conditions are met, contact weakens group boundaries and promotes more inclusive attitudes, and our intervention creates exactly these conditions

The Delivery Mechanism: Experiential Learning

Kolb’s Experiential Learning Cycle (1984)

Theory of Change

Input: Experiential learning designed for pedagogy, not gender

Mechanism: Shared experiential consumption triggers belief updating through:

  • Joint problem-solving during experiments
  • Collaborative sense-making of results
  • Equal participation in learning tasks

Dual Outcomes: Learning gains (primary goal) AND stereotype reduction (collateral benefit)

What We Expect to Find

Primary Outcome: Science Knowledge

The Pedagogical Goal

Experiential learning will improve science knowledge through:

  • Concrete experience with concepts
  • Reflective observation and discussion
  • Abstract conceptualization
  • Active experimentation and application

Added Benefit: Reduced Stereotypes

The Indirect Effect

Shared experiential consumption may reduce gender-stereotype attitudes through:

  • Cross-gender contact under optimal conditions
  • Organic exposure to counterexamples
  • Weakening of group boundaries
  • Students experiencing science as “for us”

Institutional Context

Science Education in Indian Public Schools

The System

  • 70% of students attend government schools
  • >50% of Grades 9–10 learn science without proper labs
  • Student-teacher ratios: 20:1 to 27:1

Pedagogical Approach

  • Rote learning dominates; textbook memorization
  • Only 14% of classrooms use non-textbook materials
  • Gender-segregated seating (boys on one side, girls on the other)

The Gap

Lack of hands-on, interactive learning — and no cross-gender collaboration. Exactly where experiential learning could make a difference.

A typical public-school classroom: gender-segregated, teacher-centered, no lab.

Talking Point: “Before I show you our intervention, you need to understand the context. In India, 70% of students attend government schools — the backbone of the system. But more than half are learning science without any lab facilities. Combine that with rote learning and 20-to-1 student-teacher ratios, and you have a perfect storm: memorization, zero hands-on experience, and no collaborative inquiry that would create cross-gender contact.”

Study Location: Bodoland, Assam

Geographic Context

  • Autonomous territorial region in Northeast India (Assam state)
  • Established in 2003; Bodo population and other ethnic communities
  • Complex socio-political environment

Why This Location?

  • Clean baseline - limited prior exposure to experiential learning
  • High need - underserved schools, resource constraints
  • Scalability insights - if it works here, it can work anywhere

Socioeconomic Profile

  • Widespread socioeconomic disadvantage
  • Educational underdevelopment compared to national averages
  • Predominantly rural population

Educational Context

  • Schools operate with minimal facilities
  • Students have little exposure to hands-on science
  • Traditional gender norms prevalent

Talking Point: “We conducted this study in Bodoland, an autonomous region in Northeast India. This isn’t an easy place to work. It’s economically disadvantaged, educationally underdeveloped, and has a complex history of ethnic tensions. But that’s exactly why it’s valuable. These schools have had minimal exposure to educational innovations, so we can cleanly measure our intervention’s impact. And if experiential learning works here, in resource-constrained rural schools with traditional gender norms, then it can work almost anywhere. This is about external validity and policy relevance.”

The Intervention

Mobile Science Labs (MSL) Program

Program Features

  • Vehicles equipped with lab equipment
  • Two instructors + one driver per MSL
  • Minimum 2 sessions per day

Implementation

  • 10 sessions per academic year + 2 assessment sessions
  • Subjects: Biology, Chemistry, Physics, and Mathematics
  • Aligns with NCERT and State Board syllabi

Key Feature

Mixed-gender collaborative activities — boys and girls working side-by-side on shared experiments.

Mobile Science Lab Vehicle

Happening in Bloomington Too

STEM to Them Mobile Lab

  • Operated by Monroe County Community School Corporation with local industry and foundation partners
  • Brings the same hands-on, vehicle-based science model to Bloomington-area schools

Research Design

Study Design Overview

Sample Composition

Total Sample:

  • N = 5,216 students
  • 193 public schools
  • Grades 6-8 (middle school)
  • Location: Bodoland, Assam, India

Treatment Group:

  • 4,608 students (88% of sample)
  • Received MSL intervention
  • Ten two-hour sessions over the academic year

Control Group:

  • 608 students (12% of sample)
  • Regular public school education
  • No MSL intervention

Research Design

Design Type:

  • Two-wave panel design
  • Pre-intervention (baseline)
  • Post-intervention (endline)
  • One academic year duration

Analytical Approach:

  • Difference-in-differences estimation
  • School and grade fixed effects (incl. School × Grade)
  • Clustered standard errors (school level)
  • Heterogeneity/moderation analyses

Talking Point: “Now for the research design. We worked with nearly 5,200 students across almost 200 schools in Bodoland. The treatment group (about 4,600 students) received the MSL intervention over one academic year. The control group (about 600 students) continued with regular instruction. We measured outcomes before and after the intervention, which lets us use a difference-in-differences design. This is important because it accounts for any pre-existing differences between treatment and control schools.”

Primary Outcome Measures

Science Knowledge Score

Measurement:

  • 4 multiple-choice questions per assessment
  • Grade-specific content aligned with curriculum

Scoring:

  • Percentage of items answered correctly (0-100%)
  • Enables cross-grade comparison

Validation:

  • Captures applied reasoning (not rote recall)

Gender-Stereotype Attitudes

Measurement:

  • Single-item: “I feel science is for boys more than girls”
  • 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly disagree, 5 = Strongly agree)

Interpretation:

  • Higher scores = stronger gender stereotypes

Rationale for Single Item:

  • Appropriate for children/adolescents; avoids cognitive overload
  • Established practice (Deslauriers et al. 2019; Carlana et al. 2022)

Talking Point: “Our first key outcome is science knowledge. We gave students four multiple-choice questions at the beginning and end of the year. These aren’t memorization questions. They test whether students actually understand scientific concepts and can apply them. We score them as percentage correct, which lets us compare across grades even though the specific questions differ. This measures the pedagogical benefit of experiential learning. Our second key outcome measures gender stereotypes. We asked students directly: ‘I feel science is for boys more than girls.’ Five-point scale from strongly disagree to strongly agree. This is intentionally simple and direct. Some might ask: why just one question? For children, a single clear item is actually better than a long battery of similar questions. It avoids confusion and fatigue while still capturing the core belief we care about: do you think science is gendered or not?”

Secondary Outcomes

Science-Related Attitudes:

  • Interest and enjoyment in science
  • Science self-efficacy
  • Long-term science orientation
  • Intentions to choose science major
  • 14 total constructs measured

Gendered Perceptions (Post-intervention only):

Two questions asking students to infer likely gender of a high-performing student:

  • Science domain: “A student performs very well in science. Is this student a boy or a girl?”
  • Language arts domain: “A student performs very well in language arts. Is this student a boy or a girl?”

Positive effects on 12 of 14 outcomes — the intervention shifts the broader constellation of science attitudes, not only knowledge and stereotypes.

Exploring the Mechanism

Why We Need More Than Field Evidence

The field study bundles multiple features

The MSL program combines:

  • Experiential, hands-on pedagogy
  • Mixed-gender collaborative grouping
  • Equal-status peer interaction
  • Extended exposure over several sessions

The field data cannot isolate whether stereotype reduction arises from the gender composition of groups, the hands-on nature of activities, or some combination.

Two randomized studies

1. Scenario Experiment

N = 151 US adults on Prolific. Comic-strip vignettes that manipulate the gender composition of a science task.

2. Synthetic Participant Replication

450 LLM agent surrogates with demographically diverse personas — does the pattern hold under a very different participant source?

Scenario Experiment: Design

Between-subjects, 3 conditions

Mixed-Gender — Emily & Jake collaborate equally on the experiment

Solo-Male — Jake conducts the experiment alone

Solo-Female — Emily conducts the experiment alone


  • Stimulus: comic-strip vignettes of a chemistry experiment on how temperature affects reaction rate
  • Identical in content, visual style, and length; differ only in gender composition

Sample and measures

  • N = 151 adults aged 18-30, US residents (Prolific)
  • 59.6% female, 37.1% male, 3.3% non-binary
  • Approximately balanced across conditions (n ≈ 50 each)
  • 100% passed both attention-check questions

Outcome

Composite of four stereotype items on a 5-pt Likert scale (α = .845):

  1. “Boys are naturally better at science than girls”
  2. “Science is more of a ‘boy thing’ than a ‘girl thing’”
  3. “Girls can be just as good at science as boys” (reverse-coded)
  4. “Boys tend to be more interested in science than girls”

Scenario Experiment: The Three Stimuli

Mixed-Gender (Emily & Jake)

Solo-Male (Jake)

Solo-Female (Emily)

All three vignettes depict the same chemistry experiment — temperature effects on reaction rate — with identical dialogue content; only the gender composition of the protagonists varies.

Scenario Experiment: Results

Contact beyond role models

Among female participants, Mixed-Gender (M = 1.31) scored below Solo-Female (M = 1.49) — contact reduces stereotypes above and beyond counter-stereotypical exemplars (d = −0.63, p = .021).

Synthetic Participant Replication: Setup

450 LLM agent surrogates (150 per condition)

Building on the homo silicus framework (Horton 2023) and recent marketing work on GenAI-assisted research (Arora et al. 2025; Blanchard et al. 2025).

Design

  • Model: Claude Sonnet API (fixed version, controlled temperature)
  • Personas: each agent varies in age, gender, race, academic major, and science attitudes
  • Stimuli: text-based versions of the same three vignettes

Two notable differences vs. human study

  • Stereotype items use projective framing to reduce social-desirability bias (Luchs et al. 2010)
  • Vignettes delivered as text, not comic strips (API-based execution)

Synthetic Participant Replication: Results

Same pattern across participant sources

The Mixed-Gender < Solo-Male ordering replicates with 450 LLM agents (d = 0.65, p < .001). Treat this as a directional robustness check, not independent causal evidence — LLM agents may reflect training-data regularities rather than genuine psychological processes.

Discussion

Theoretical Contributions

Marketing theory

  • Extends shared consumption and social learning from commercial to educational settings
  • Frames belief change as symbolic brand repositioning — who belongs in a domain
  • Experience-based, not persuasion-based, identity change

Education & gender equity

  • Spotlights boys and peer environments as central levers for stereotype reduction
  • Complements a literature focused on interventions targeting girls
  • Boys who update their beliefs grow into male teachers, managers, and colleagues

Service design

  • Indirect change as a design principle: redesign the experience, not the message
  • Dual-outcome design (knowledge + attitudes) from a single program
  • Equal-status, mixed-gender collaboration as a replicable service feature

Policy Implications

For NGOs & policymakers

  • Experiential learning is a scalable lever — no identity-targeted programming needed
  • Investment in lab access + facilitator training has dual returns
  • Works across class compositions (no ratio engineering required)

For corporate funders

  • High-leverage CSR channel: one program delivers education outcomes and gender equity KPIs
  • Metrics align with UN SDG reporting
  • Measurable downstream impact on the STEM pipeline

For school leaders

  • Pedagogy is the intervention — everyday classroom design choices matter
  • Mixed-gender grouping on shared tasks is low-cost, high-signal
  • Gains are larger in larger schools where norms are most salient

Alignment with UN SDGs

SDG 4 — Quality Education

  • Hands-on, experiential science instruction
  • Measurable improvement in standardized knowledge

SDG 5 — Gender Equality

  • Stereotype reductions during formative years
  • Reshaping who belongs in science upstream of the STEM pipeline leak

A single service-design intervention advances two SDGs simultaneously — dual-impact evidence donors and multilateral agencies look for.

Field Study — Primary Outcome Measures

Gender-Stereotype Attitude (primary attitudinal outcome)

Single-item measure administered at baseline and endline:

“I feel science is for boys more than girls.”

  • 5-point Likert scale
  • 1 = Strongly Disagree · · · 5 = Strongly Agree
  • Higher scores = stronger stereotype endorsement
  • N = 10,214 students with complete responses across two waves

Item is a concrete, single-referent belief — the type of construct for which single-item measurement is appropriate (Bergkvist & Rossiter 2007).

Science Knowledge Score

  • Standardized, grade-appropriate science test
  • Covers chemistry, physics, and biology content aligned with the state curriculum
  • Scored as a percentage correct
  • N = 10,432 students with complete responses across two waves
  • Administered at baseline (pre-intervention) and endline (post-intervention)

Field Study — Broader Science Attitudes (Secondary)

14 items across four conceptual categories

1. Interest & enjoyment of science

  • I enjoy doing science activities
  • Science is one of my favorite subjects
  • I look forward to science class
  • I find science interesting

2. Self-efficacy in science

  • I feel confident solving science problems
  • I can understand difficult science concepts
  • I am good at science

3. Career intentions in STEM

  • I want to have a career that uses science
  • I would like to be a scientist / engineer / doctor
  • I can picture myself working in a science field

4. Perceived social value of science

  • Science helps solve important problems
  • My community values science
  • Science is useful in everyday life
  • Understanding science helps me make good decisions

All items: 5-pt Likert (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree).

Field Study — Gendered Perception of Academic Excellence

Projective-style post-intervention item

Respondents were asked, for each of two domains:

“A student performs very well in [science / language arts]. Is this student a boy or a girl?”

  • Response options: Boy · Girl · Could be either
  • Administered separately for science excellence and language arts excellence
  • Outcome coded to capture share of students attributing excellence more equally across genders (“could be either” or balanced responses)
  • Language-arts version serves as a placebo check — does the intervention shift gender attributions only in the targeted domain?

Scenario Experiment — Stereotype Composite

Four items (5-pt Likert; α = .845)

  1. “Boys are naturally better at science than girls.”
  2. “Science is more of a ‘boy thing’ than a ‘girl thing.’”
  3. “Girls can be just as good at science as boys.” (reverse-coded)
  4. “Boys tend to be more interested in science than girls.”

Scoring:

  • Scale: 1 = Strongly Disagree · · · 5 = Strongly Agree
  • Item 3 reverse-coded; then all four averaged into a composite
  • Higher composite = stronger stereotype endorsement

Sample & procedure

  • N = 151 adults aged 18–30 recruited on Prolific
  • US residents with at least secondary education
  • 59.6% female, 37.1% male, 3.3% non-binary
  • Approximately balanced across 3 conditions (n ≈ 50 each)
  • Participants viewed one of three comic-strip vignettes (Mixed-Gender / Solo-Male / Solo-Female), then completed two attention checks before the stereotype items

Scenario Experiment — Attention Checks

Two comprehension checks verifying the vignette was processed

Attention Check 1

“In the comic strip you just saw, what were the student(s) doing?”

  • A math problem
  • Reading a book
  • A chemistry experiment ← correct
  • Playing a sport

Attention Check 2

“In the comic strip you just saw, who was/were the main character(s)?”

  • Condition-appropriate names (Emily, Jake, or both) as the correct option, with foils


  • 100% pass rate on both checks across all three conditions
  • Stereotype items administered only after both attention checks
  • Stimuli reproduced in Web Appendix G of the paper

EDSL Synthetic Replication — Agent Construction

Persona dimensions (each agent unique)

  • Age (18–30 distribution matching Prolific sample)
  • Gender (balanced across male, female, non-binary)
  • Race/ethnicity (US census-matched distribution)
  • Academic major (STEM / non-STEM / undeclared)
  • Baseline science attitudes (low / moderate / high)

Stimuli

  • Text-based descriptions of the same three conditions (Mixed-Gender / Solo-Male / Solo-Female)
  • Vignette text parallels the comic-strip dialogue
  • Each agent assigned to one condition (150 per condition, N = 450)

Execution best practices (Blanchard et al. 2025)

  • Model: Claude Sonnet via Anthropic API
  • API-based execution (not chat interface)
  • Fixed model version
  • Temperature controlled
  • Complete response logging (including agent-generated open-ended reasoning)

Item framing difference vs. human study

  • Stereotype items delivered with projective framing (third-person phrasing; Luchs et al. 2010) to reduce social-desirability bias in LLM responses
  • Otherwise item content mirrors the human scenario experiment

Full methodology in Web Appendix H of the paper.
